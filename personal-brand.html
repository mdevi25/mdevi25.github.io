The problem

Most professionals have a LinkedIn profile but no owned space that reflects how they actually think. A static CV lists titles and dates. It doesn't show frameworks, perspective, intellectual range, or what someone is like to work with.

The alternative — hiring a developer, buying a template, learning a web framework — felt like solving the wrong problem. The real challenge was not technical. It was deciding what to say and being specific enough that it sounds like you, not a polished version of you.

The site should give before it asks. Every visitor should walk away with something useful, even if they never reach out.

The guiding principle

The site had to do three things well:

  • Reflect genuine thinking — not a LinkedIn summary, but actual frameworks, real opinions, and honest perspective on AI and product management.
  • Give value on arrival — a calendar with 30 AI concept cards and a 12-step Beginner's Journey means every visitor learns something, whether or not they ever contact you.
  • Stay maintainable — adding a new project, article, or travel destination should take one conversation, not an afternoon of editing code.

Key product decisions

Decision 1
GitHub Pages over Webflow, Framer, or Squarespace
No domain purchase, no subscription, no vendor lock-in. GitHub Pages gives a permanent free URL and keeps all files in your control. The constraint of a single HTML file turned out to be a feature — simplicity forces clarity.
Decision 2
Conversation over prompts
Every change was an iterative conversation, not a one-shot prompt. This is the key insight: AI collaboration works best when you treat it like working with a capable colleague — context, feedback, and refinement over time.
Decision 3
A hidden gem over a contact form
The calendar with 30 AI concept cards and a 12-step Beginner's Journey was a deliberate product decision — give visitors something useful on arrival. A site that teaches earns more trust than a site that only asks.
Decision 4
Perspective widget over a generic chatbot
Rather than a generic AI assistant, the widget is grounded in actual frameworks, real writing, and defined opinions. Visitors get a genuine point of view — not a search engine or a placeholder.

Key takeaways

1
The hardest part is content, not code. Deciding what to say — and being specific enough that it sounds like you — is where the real work is. AI handles the execution. The thinking is yours.
2
AI collaboration is a product skill. Knowing what to ask for, when to push back, when to approve, and when to redirect — that is product management applied to your own build. The output quality mirrors the quality of your direction.
3
Specificity is everything. Vague inputs produce generic outputs. The moments where the site feels most like me are the moments where I gave Claude the most specific brief — actual frameworks, real opinions, exact wording preferences.
4
A living portfolio beats a static CV. The site continues to grow — new projects, new articles, new travel destinations — each added in a single conversation. This is the right model for a professional in a fast-moving field.
